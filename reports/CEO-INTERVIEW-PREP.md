## A1. "What do you understand about our regulatory positioning?"

**Hook:** "I think regulatory architecture is the single most important thing about Perpetuals -- and the thing most people overlook."

**Core:** You hold a CySEC MTF License -- #458/25 -- which puts you in the same regulatory class as traditional stock exchanges, not crypto exchanges. That license gives you MiFID II passporting across 30 EEA countries from a single jurisdiction. You're already compliant with DORA, you're ahead of MiCA enforcement in July, and Ledgera's T+0 settlement exceeds the EU T+1 mandate that doesn't even take effect until October 2027.

**Data point:** I mapped the regulatory timeline on the Market Thesis page I built -- DORA already live, MiCA in July 2026, EMIR 3.0 in June 2026, EU T+1 in October 2027. Each of those deadlines is a filter that eliminates competitors. There are 100+ exchanges today. After those deadlines, maybe fewer than 10 survive. Perpetuals passes every gate.

**Tie-back:** "That's not a legal advantage -- it's a product advantage. Every regulation you've already cleared is a feature your competitors can't ship."

## A2. "Why do Barrier Futures matter -- and who are they for?"

**Hook:** "$150 billion in forced liquidations happened in 2025 alone. Barrier Futures make that number zero for your users."

**Core:** Traditional perpetual swaps have cascading liquidations -- one position gets liquidated, which pushes the price further, which liquidates the next position, and it dominoes. Barrier Futures eliminate that entirely. The position closes at the barrier. No margin calls, no funding fees, no cascades. Same leveraged exposure, fundamentally different risk profile.

**Data point:** ESMA data shows 74-89% of retail CFD/derivative accounts lose money. Regulators are watching this closely. Barrier Futures aren't just better for traders -- they're better for the regulatory story. They make leveraged products that regulators can actually endorse.

**Tie-back:** "The audience is two-sided. For brokers, it's a product they can offer without the reputation risk of liquidation cascades. For retail traders, it's leveraged exposure without the nightmare scenario. And for regulators, it's a derivatives product that actually protects retail users. That's rare."

**Tie-back:** "The difference is that you're not building a product, you're building an institution. That's a Patrick Gruhn thesis, and it's the right one. The question is whether the user experience communicates that same level of institutional seriousness."

## B1. "How do you think about design for a product most people don't understand yet?"

**Hook:** "You don't simplify the product -- you simplify the path into the product."

**Core:** Progressive disclosure. That's the framework. A first-time user seeing Barrier Futures doesn't need to understand barrier mechanics, funding rate elimination, or cascade prevention on day one. They need to understand: "I can take a leveraged position, and if it goes against me, it closes automatically at a price I set. No surprise liquidation." That's one sentence. The depth comes later, as the user's sophistication grows.

**Example:** I did this at CloudCar with automotive infotainment for Jaguar Land Rover. The underlying system had hundreds of controls -- HVAC, navigation, media, vehicle settings, ADAS. The design challenge wasn't reducing functionality. It was creating layers: surface-level controls for driving, deeper menus for parked configuration. Same principle applies to trading interfaces. The complexity exists. The design question is when and how to reveal it.

**Tie-back:** "Barrier Futures is actually an easier design challenge than traditional perpetual swaps because the product itself is simpler. The UX should match that simplicity. The hard part is educating users who already think they understand perpetual swaps that this is fundamentally different."

## B2. "What's your philosophy on trust in financial interfaces?"

**Hook:** "Trust comes from three layers: transparency, consistency, and intentional friction."

**Core:** Transparency means the user always knows the state of their money -- no ambiguous statuses, no hidden fees, no mystery processing states. Consistency means the interface behaves predictably enough that users build muscle memory -- the confirmation flow works the same whether you're trading $100 or $100,000. Intentional friction means the system slows you down at moments where the cost of a mistake is high -- confirmation dialogs on large orders, two-factor on withdrawals, cooling periods on risk limit changes.

**Example:** The insight I landed on during my competitive research -- and this became the thesis of the showcase I built -- is that friction is a design tool, not a bug. Every consumer app is racing to zero friction. In professional trading, you need friction. The question is where you put it. A fat-finger error on a $2 million order at 3am needs a confirmation step. A routine market buy on a small position doesn't.

**Tie-back:** "For Perpetuals specifically, trust has an extra dimension: you're asking users to trust a platform with FTX in its lineage. That makes every trust signal in the interface 10x more important. The design has to communicate institutional seriousness at every touchpoint."

## B3. "How do you balance simplicity with the complexity our institutional users need?"

**Hook:** "You don't balance them -- you layer them. The same platform can serve both if the information architecture is right."

**Core:** Institutional users and retail traders don't need different products. They need different default views of the same product. A broker onboarding to Kronos X needs API documentation, matching engine specs, and compliance frameworks on first contact. A retail trader using Barrier Futures needs a clean order entry form and a clear risk indicator. Both users are on the same platform -- the design question is which layer they enter at and how deep they can drill.

**Example:** Bloomberg solves this by being entirely institutional -- they don't serve retail at all. Robinhood solves it by being entirely retail. The platforms that try to do both -- Kraken, Interactive Brokers -- tend to default to one audience and frustrate the other. In my competitive analysis, I identified this as the open quadrant: institutional depth with consumer clarity. Nobody occupies that space. That's where Perpetuals should be.

**Tie-back:** "The product stack supports this naturally. Kronos X is the institutional layer. Barrier Futures is the retail-accessible layer. BayesShield serves both. The design system just needs to surface the right layer to the right user at the right time."

## D1. "How would you design the onboarding flow for Barrier Futures?"

**Hook:** "The biggest design challenge isn't explaining Barrier Futures. It's un-teaching what people already know about perpetual swaps."

**Core:** Most traders coming to Barrier Futures will have mental models from traditional perpetual swaps -- margin, funding rates, liquidation levels. Barrier Futures removes all of that. So the onboarding has to do two things simultaneously: explain the new product and explicitly address what's different. I'd use a comparison-first approach -- "Here's what you're used to. Here's what's different. Here's why."

**Specific approach:**
1. **Entry screen:** One visual comparison -- traditional perp vs Barrier Future. Side-by-side cascade visualization (I actually built this on the Market Thesis page -- red bars cascading down vs green bars holding steady).
2. **Barrier selection:** The key UX moment. The user needs to understand that the barrier IS their risk limit. I'd use a slider with real-time P&L simulation -- "If BTC drops to this price, your position closes. Your maximum loss is $X."
3. **Confirmation:** Show the three things that are NOT happening -- no margin calls, no funding fees, no liquidation cascades. Frame the absence as the feature.
4. **Post-trade education:** After the first trade settles, surface a 30-second explainer. Users learn better after they've done the thing, not before.

**Tie-back:** "The design has to make Barrier Futures feel safer AND simpler than what traders are used to -- because it is. If the onboarding makes it feel more complicated, we've failed."

## D2. "We serve both institutional brokers and retail traders. How do you design one platform for both?"

**Hook:** "Two entry points, one platform, shared design system."

**Core:** Institutional brokers and retail traders have fundamentally different first-contact experiences. **Framework:**
- **Shared foundation:** Design tokens, typography, color system, component library, interaction patterns
- **Institutional surface:** Data-dense dashboards, multi-asset views, risk management panels, compliance reporting
- **Retail surface:** Clean order entry, simplified portfolio view, educational overlays, progressive disclosure

**Tie-back:** "This is actually the insight I mapped in the competitive analysis -- the positioning chart. Bloomberg owns institutional complexity. Robinhood owns retail simplicity. The open quadrant is institutional depth with consumer clarity. That's a design system problem, not a product problem. And it's solvable."

## D3. "BayesShield tells a trader their trade is likely to lose. How do you present that without losing the user?"

**Hook:** "You frame it as intelligence, not judgment. The system isn't telling you you're wrong. It's giving you data most traders don't have."

**Core:** BayesShield trained on 11.7 billion trades and can filter 92% of losing positions. That's an extraordinary signal. But if you surface it as "WARNING: this trade will lose money," you'll get two reactions -- angry traders who feel patronized, and paranoid traders who think the platform is trading against them. Neither is good.

**Design approach:**
- **Risk score, not a verdict.** "BayesShield risk signal: elevated" is better than "BayesShield says you'll lose." Show a gradient, not a binary.
- **Historical context.** "Trades with similar parameters had a 73% loss rate in the last 30 days." That's a data point the user can act on, not a command.
- **Opt-in depth.** Surface the signal. Let the user tap/click for the reasoning. Don't block the trade -- friction, not prohibition.
- **Regulatory framing.** "This risk intelligence is part of Perpetuals' commitment to investor protection under MiFID II." Now it's a compliance feature and a trust signal.

**Tie-back:** "BayesShield is actually a massive differentiator -- no one else has this. The design challenge is making it feel like a premium feature, not a nanny. The 92% accuracy number should make traders feel like they have an edge, not like they're being restricted."

## D4. "How would you approach making our compliance and regulatory status visible to users without cluttering the interface?"

**Hook:** "Compliance should be ambient -- always visible, never in the way."

**Core:** Three tiers of compliance visibility:
1. **Always present:** A small, persistent trust badge in the header or sidebar -- "CySEC Licensed | MTF #458/25" -- similar to how SSL certificates work. It's there. You see it. You don't have to interact with it.
2. **Contextual:** When a user is about to make a trade, deposit funds, or change risk settings -- surface relevant regulatory protections. "Your funds are held in segregated accounts under CySEC regulation." This isn't clutter, it's reassurance at the moment of highest anxiety.
3. **On demand:** A dedicated compliance hub -- licenses, audit results, regulatory status, jurisdiction coverage. For institutional clients, this is a purchasing decision page. For retail users, it's a safety net they can check when they need peace of mind.

**Tie-back:** "The Perpetuals story is that regulation is a competitive advantage. The interface should tell that story. Not with paragraphs of legal text, but with persistent, elegant trust signals that become part of the brand experience."

**Q7. "Kronos X serves institutional MTFs and eventually retail users. At what point does the platform need to fork into separate experiences, and who makes that call?"**
*Why this works:* Product architecture disguised as a design question. Shows you're thinking about platform strategy, not just screens.

### About Upcoming Challenges

**Q8. "Barrier Futures launches in March. What's the biggest user experience concern you have about the launch?"**
*Why this works:* Direct, timely, and gives you an immediate contribution opportunity. If he answers, you can follow up with ideas.

### Bold Questions (showing confidence)

**Q9. "What's the hardest thing about Barrier Futures that you haven't solved yet?"**
*Why this works:* Most candidates would never ask a CEO what's unsolved. It shows intellectual confidence and genuine curiosity. He'll respect the directness.

**Q10. "If you could hire one more person tomorrow -- not a designer, anyone -- who would it be and why?"**
*Why this works:* Reveals his actual priorities and pain points. If he says "a regulatory expert," you know where the pressure is. If he says "a designer," you know you're a high priority.
