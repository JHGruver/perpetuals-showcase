Phase A — Discover
01

Research & Discovery

Before I designed a single screen, I needed to understand what makes trading apps hard. Not just hard for beginners — hard for everyone. I spent two weeks in the problem space before opening Figma.

Stakeholder Interviews
Talked to the product team, engineering leads, and compliance. Key insight: the regulatory advantage (CySEC MTF license #458/25 — same classification as traditional stock exchanges) needs to be felt in the UI, not just stated in marketing. MiFID II passporting gives access to 30 EEA countries. Compliance is a feature, not a constraint.
What I learnedThe compliance team told me something I didn’t expect: their biggest competitive advantage isn’t speed or fees, it’s that they’re the only MTF-regulated exchange offering Barrier Futures. No competitor in the encyclopedia’s competitive matrix has this combination. Backpack EU has a similar FTX lineage but was fined €200K by CySEC. That regulatory moat needs to be visible everywhere. Source: CySEC, Perpetuals Product Encyclopedia.
Domain Research
Spent a week learning crypto derivatives from scratch. Order types, margin mechanics, funding rates, liquidation cascades. The global OTC derivatives market is $846T (BIS, June 2025). Crypto derivatives alone: $85.7T annually, $264.5B daily average. In 2025, $150B was lost to forced liquidations (CoinGlass). These aren’t abstract numbers — they’re the design problem.
What I learnedThe single biggest UX problem in derivatives trading is liquidation anxiety. $150B in forced liquidations in 2025 alone (CoinGlass). ESMA data shows 74–89% of retail CFD traders lose money. Traders don’t fear losing money — they fear losing more than they intended. That’s exactly what Barrier Futures solve: your max loss is defined upfront, zero margin calls, zero funding fees. Sources: BIS Triennial Survey, CoinGlass, ESMA.
Technical Constraints
Mobile-first means 375px viewport, thumb-zone navigation, intermittent connectivity. Real-time price data needs WebSocket streams updating every 100ms without jank. The chart has to be as responsive as the one on desktop.
What I learnedMost trading apps are desktop-first with a mobile port. The ones that go mobile-first (Robinhood) win on simplicity but lose on power. APPXISH needs to thread that needle — simple by default, powerful on demand.
Market Sizing
Crypto derivatives daily average: $264.5B (CoinGlass). BlackRock IBIT AUM: $54B (Feb 2026). CME crypto contracts/day record: 794,903 (+132% YoY). Tokenized assets market: $2.08T. Vision 2030 TAM: $50T, targeting 1B users across 100+ countries. But the real opportunity is the 70%+ of potential traders who bounce because the UX is hostile.
What I learnedThat bounce rate isn’t a marketing problem — it’s a design problem. People download Binance, see the trading panel, and close the app. BlackRock putting $54B into a Bitcoin ETF (IBIT, Feb 2026) proves institutional demand is here. CME’s +132% YoY crypto growth proves momentum. If APPXISH can convert even 5% of those bouncers, it’s a billion-dollar difference. Sources: CoinGlass, BlackRock, CME Group, Perpetuals Vision 2030.
User Recruitment
Recruited 12 participants across four segments: crypto beginners (4), active traders migrating from competitors (3), institutional/professional (2), and brokers (3). Mix of genders, ages 22-45, across US, EU, and APAC.

Competitive UX Audit

I spent a weekend actually trading on every one of these platforms. Not just screenshots — real money, real frustration. Here’s what I found and what we can steal or avoid.

Binance
6.5/10
  • Deepest liquidity, most trading pairs
  • Advanced order types for power users
  • Comprehensive API ecosystem
  • Mobile UI is a direct port of desktop — overwhelming
  • Onboarding is a 15-minute KYC gauntlet
  • Regulatory uncertainty undermines trust
Design takeawayBinance proves that power users will tolerate bad UX for good liquidity. But they’re hemorrhaging beginners. Our opportunity: match the depth, beat the onboarding.
Bybit
7.0/10
  • Cleaner mobile experience than Binance
  • Copy trading feature is clever
  • Good chart integration (TradingView)
  • Leverage UX is dangerous — too easy to set 100x
  • Risk warnings are buried in legal copy
  • No Barrier Futures or equivalent
Design takeawayBybit’s leverage slider is my anti-pattern. There’s no friction between 5x and 100x. That’s not user-friendly, it’s reckless. APPXISH needs to make high leverage feel heavy.
dYdX
6.0/10
  • Decentralized — appeals to crypto-native users
  • Clean, minimalist interface
  • Wallet connection UX is confusing for beginners
  • No fiat on-ramp — you need crypto to start
  • Limited order types
Robinhood
8.5/10
  • Best-in-class onboarding (account in 3 minutes)
  • Mobile-first design is genuinely beautiful
  • Progressive disclosure done right
  • No derivatives / futures trading
  • Oversimplified — power users outgrow it
  • Gamification led to $7.5M settlement, SEC inquiry (confetti, scratch-offs)
Design takeawayRobinhood is the gold standard for beginner UX (2015 Apple Design Award, 2016 Google Play Award). Their progressive disclosure model is what I’m adapting for APPXISH. But their gamification crossed the line: $7.5M settlement, Massachusetts regulatory action, SEC industry inquiry into gamified investing. UKGC RTS 14F prohibits celebrating returns ≤ the stake. We take the simplicity, lose the confetti. Source: SEC, CNBC, Gambling UX Research.
Coinbase
7.5/10
  • Trust factor is extremely high
  • Excellent educational content
  • Clean, professional aesthetic
  • No perpetual futures product
  • Advanced features feel bolted on
  • Fee structure is confusing
Kraken
6.5/10
  • Strong regulatory standing
  • Good security track record
  • Futures product exists
  • Mobile app feels dated
  • Two separate apps (Kraken / Kraken Pro)
  • Futures UX is clunky

Adjacent Industry UX Audit

The best ideas don’t come from copying competitors — they come from adjacent industries solving the same UX problem in a different context.

FTMO
7.5/10
  • Clean evaluation dashboard with progress tracking
  • Risk metrics (drawdown, profit targets) always visible
  • Account scaling UX makes progression feel rewarding
  • Challenge purchase flow could be simpler
  • Mobile experience is secondary to desktop
Why a prop firm mattersFTMO’s evaluation model — where traders operate within defined loss limits — is conceptually identical to Barrier Futures. Their drawdown visualization is something we should study closely. The way they present “you can lose up to X” is the exact UI pattern we need.
FundingPips
7.0/10
  • Modern, clean dashboard design
  • Challenge purchase flow is streamlined
  • Good mobile responsiveness
  • Risk visualization less detailed than FTMO
  • Analytics depth could be stronger
Design takeawayFundingPips proves you can make complex trading rules feel accessible to retail users. Their onboarding simplicity is worth stealing — they don’t front-load complexity, they reveal it progressively.
Alpha Capital Group
6.5/10
  • Institutional-quality risk dashboards
  • Professional analytics and reporting
  • Strong account management UX
  • UI feels more B2B than consumer
  • Mobile experience is an afterthought
DraftKings / FanDuel
8.0/10
  • Progressive information density — featured events → full markets → live micro-markets
  • “Hover to reveal” for secondary actions reduces visual clutter at rest
  • Persistent bet slip as bottom sheet on mobile — the exact order panel pattern we need
  • Real-time odds with 1–3s color flashes for movement (not persistent coloring)
  • Dark patterns around deposits and bonus wagering
  • Risk communication is intentionally vague
  • 3+ promotional notifications/hour causes disengagement (gambling UX research)
What sports betting teaches usDraftKings/FanDuel solve the hardest UX problem in betting: hundreds of real-time markets without overwhelming casual users. Their bet slip pattern (sticky, real-time payout calc, preserves state across navigation) maps directly to a persistent order panel. Onboarding converts at 80%+ because the first bet is frictionless. We want that same energy for the first Barrier trade — without the dark patterns. Source: Gambling × Trading UX Research.
eToro / Plus500
6.0/10
  • Social trading / copy trading features
  • ESMA-compliant risk disclosures baked into UI
  • Leverage selector with mandatory warnings
  • CFD broker complexity leaks through simplified UI
  • Risk warnings feel like legal CYA, not user education
The MiFID II lessoneToro and Plus500 live under the same MiFID II regime we’ll operate in. Their risk disclosure UX is a preview of what regulators expect — but their approach is “checkbox compliance.” We can do better by making risk communication genuinely useful, not just legally sufficient.
Polymarket / Kalshi
7.0/10
  • ~$3.5B peak monthly volume (Dec 2025) — proves consumer-web aesthetic scales
  • Probability percentages as primary metric — more intuitive than prices or odds
  • Binary Yes/No simplicity reduces interface to two large buttons
  • Category-based discovery (Politics, Sports, Crypto) reduces cognitive load
  • Limited depth for sophisticated traders — no advanced order types
  • Liquidity thin on niche markets
  • No institutional features (no API, no FIX, no co-location)
Betfair Exchange
8.5/10
  • Full order book with back (buy, blue) / lay (sell, pink) columns
  • Price ladder with click-to-populate — identical to DOM trading
  • Position in Queue (PIQ) shows how close to fill
  • Multi-market grids: 4–8 markets simultaneously
  • Learning curve is steep for non-betting users
  • UI feels dated compared to consumer apps
The most relevant referenceBetfair Exchange is functionally a trading platform wearing a betting skin. Their two-color directional system (blue/pink, not green/red) instantly communicates directionality. The price ladder with click-to-populate is the gold standard for active trading — the exact DOM interface Kronos X needs. Their third-party API ecosystem (Bet Angel, betfairviz) is what our API-first architecture should enable. Source: Gambling × Trading UX Research, Betfair API documentation.

User Personas

Who are we actually designing for? These four people represent very different relationships with trading, risk, and technology. Every design decision should trace back to one of them.

Alex Chen
Retail Beginner
Age: 24 — Recent college grad, crypto-curious
Goals: First derivatives trade, understand leverage without losing everything
Pain points: Overwhelmed by complex UIs, afraid of liquidation ($150B lost in 2025), confused by funding rates
Tech comfort: High (apps), Low (finance)
Key tasks: Simple buy/sell, check P&L, withdraw gains
Entry point: Barrier Futures via Upside Only brand — mobile-first, card-based UI, progressive complexity, bold colors. No margin/liquidation complexity for beginners.
Design implicationAlex is the reason Barrier Futures matters so much from a UX perspective. ESMA data: 74–89% of retail CFD traders lose money. Traditional perps require understanding margin, liquidation, funding — that's a wall. Barriers collapse it into one question: “how much are you willing to lose?” Upside Only is Perpetuals’ consumer brand specifically for this persona — simplified frontend to the same Kronos X engine. Gambling UX research confirms demo missions increase retention 43% for users like Alex. Paper trading should be his default first experience.
Sarah Miller
Active Trader
Age: 32 — 5+ years trading experience, migrating from Binance
Goals: Better execution (<1ms latency), lower fees, regulated platform she can trust post-FTX
Pain points: Slow order fills, lack of advanced order types, counterparty risk since FTX collapse
Tech comfort: High (both apps and finance)
Key tasks: Multi-order management, chart analysis, rapid execution
Entry point: Full Perpetuals trading panel — CySEC MTF #458/25 badge visible, Kronos X engine (1–2M+ orders/sec)
Design implicationSarah already knows how Binance works. She’ll judge us by what we do differently. Kronos X: <1ms latency, 1–2M+ orders/sec, 99.99% uptime, 24/7/365. CySEC MTF license means same regulatory class as traditional stock exchanges — not just a “crypto license.” MiFID II passporting across 30 EEA countries. The win is making this regulatory moat feel tangible in the UI: proof of reserves badge, license numbers, SOC 2 Type II certification visible in settings. Source: Perpetuals Product Encyclopedia.
David Park
Professional / Institutional
Age: 41 — Prop desk trader, manages $50M+ portfolio
Goals: API access, FIX protocol support, prime brokerage, DMA (direct market access)
Pain points: Counterparty risk, slow settlement (wants T+0), regulatory compliance burden
Tech comfort: Expert (builds custom trading systems, co-locates at Equinix)
Key tasks: Large block trades, cross-margin, portfolio risk monitoring via BayesShield
Entry point: Desktop Perpetuals platform, Equinix FR2 co-location (Frankfurt), Kronos X <50µs DMA latency
Design implicationDavid barely uses the mobile app. He’s on desktop with three monitors at his co-located desk (Equinix FR2, Frankfurt). Kronos X gives him <50µs DMA latency and FIX protocol for his custom systems. Ledgera settles T+0, beating the EU T+1 mandate (Oct 2027) by 2+ years. BayesShield AI (trained on 11.7B trades, 92% losing-trade filter rate) monitors his portfolio risk in real-time. When he opens APPXISH at dinner, he needs positions, P&L, and risk alerts in a 10-second check-in. Source: Product Encyclopedia.
Maria Santos
Broker / Introducing Broker
Age: 38 — Runs a network of 200+ retail clients
Goals: Client management dashboard, commission tracking, white-label via Perpetuals infrastructure
Pain points: Fragmented platforms, manual reporting, client onboarding friction, settlement delays
Tech comfort: Moderate (business tools, not engineering)
Key tasks: Monitor client activity, manage sub-accounts, generate reports, process withdrawals via PerpetualPay
Entry point: Broker portal — Ledgera T+0 settlement, PerpetualPay for client fund management
Design implicationMaria is the reason the app needs a completely separate broker portal view. She doesn’t trade — she manages 200+ people who trade. PerpetualPay handles client deposits/withdrawals. Ledgera settles T+0 (instant cross-chain), so her clients see funds immediately — no more “pending 3-5 business days.” Her dashboard needs aggregate performance, client onboarding funnels, and commission breakdowns. Same Kronos X engine, different shell. Source: Product Encyclopedia (Brands section).

06

Design Principles

These are the rules I hold myself to. When I’m stuck on a design decision, I come back to these six. They’re opinionated on purpose — good constraints make better design.

01
Clarity Before Speed
Never sacrifice understanding for fewer taps. A user who understands what they’re doing in 3 taps is better than one confused in 1.
Do: Show a clear order summary before confirmation
Don’t: One-tap trade with no review step for beginners
Why this firstESMA data shows 74–89% of retail CFD accounts lose money. Every exchange optimizes for speed — but speed without understanding is how people accidentally set 100x leverage and lose their savings. Clarity is the foundation everything else is built on. Source: ESMA Decision (EU) 2018/796 — retail CFD loss rates across EU brokers.
02
Risk Should Be Visible
If money is on the line, the user should feel the weight of the decision. Use color, size, and motion to make risk tangible. UKGC RTS 14F requires “clear, accurate and not misleading” risk displays — we apply this to derivatives, not just betting.
Do: Leverage slider shifts to red at high multipliers, real-time liquidation price always visible
Don’t: Bury liquidation price in fine print below the fold (a $150B/year forced-liquidation problem)
The responsible design lensGambling platforms use progressive information density: critical risk info shrinks as engagement deepens. We invert this — risk info gets MORE prominent as leverage increases. Inspired by UKGC RTS 14F requirements adapted for derivatives. Sources: CoinGlass liquidation data ($150B annual), UKGC Remote Technical Standards.
03
Mobile Thumb First
Every critical action must be reachable with one thumb in the bottom 60% of the screen. Navigation at the bottom, confirmations at the bottom, cancel at the top.
Do: Buy/Sell buttons fixed at bottom of order form
Don’t: Place “Submit Order” at the top of a scrollable form
04
Progressive Complexity
Start simple, reveal power. Beginners see Market/Limit/Barrier. Power users find Stop-limit, OCO, and trailing stops one tap deeper.
Do: 3 order types visible, “More” button for advanced
Don’t: Show 8 order types in a horizontal scroll on first visit
The Robinhood lessonRobinhood nailed progressive disclosure for stocks — Apple Design Award 2015, Google Material Design Award 2016. But they also paid $7.5M (FINRA, 2024) for oversimplifying options risk. The challenge is doing the same for derivatives without hiding risk. We borrow DraftKings’ “hover-to-reveal” stat depth: beginners see Market/Limit/Barrier, power users find OCO, trailing stops, and advanced order types one tap deeper. Sources: Robinhood FINRA settlement 2024, DraftKings progressive disclosure pattern.
05
Trust Through Transparency
Show the math. Show the fees. Show the regulatory status. Post-FTX, users don’t trust any exchange by default. Earn it with data — CySEC MTF license #458/25, MiFID II passporting across 30 EEA countries, Ledgera T+0 settlement proof.
Do: Show fee breakdown before order confirmation, display CySEC license badge in footer
Don’t: Say “low fees” without showing the actual number, or hide regulatory status in a sub-page
Why regulation is a UX assetBackpack Exchange’s €200K fine from EU regulators (2025) shows what happens when compliance is an afterthought. Our CySEC MTF license and MiFID II passporting aren’t just legal requirements — they’re trust badges. Display them prominently. Sources: CySEC MTF License #458/25, ESMA MiFID II framework, Backpack Exchange enforcement action.
06
Delight Without Distraction
Animations should communicate, not decorate. A green flash on a fill means “success.” Confetti means “we don’t take your money seriously.”
Do: Subtle haptic feedback when an order fills, color-flash price ticks (Stake.com pattern)
Don’t: Celebratory confetti animation on trade execution (Robinhood controversy, 2021)
The confetti lineGambling platforms use dopamine-triggering celebrations — Stake.com’s neon-dark UI pulsates with win animations. Robinhood’s confetti on first trade was criticized by Congress. We take Stake’s real-time color flash feedback (price moves = green/red pulse) but reject the celebration loops. Feedback yes, dopamine manipulation no. Sources: Stake.com UI analysis, US House Financial Services Committee hearing on gamification (2021).

ROI / Business Case

Design isn’t just about making things pretty. Every screen I build should move a business number. Here’s my best estimate of which numbers these designs move.

-40%
Onboarding Drop-off
Streamlined KYC flow with progress indicators and demo mode reduces abandonment from ~65% to ~25%. Each recovered user = ~$2,400 lifetime value. Gambling UX research shows demo missions boost 7-day retention by 43%.
How I got this numberIndustry average KYC completion on mobile is 35%. Robinhood hits 75% with their simplified flow. Demo mode (inspired by DraftKings’ free-play challenges) gives users a risk-free first experience while KYC processes in background — gambling platforms see 40–60% of DAU from demo/free-play modes. We target 75% completion by borrowing this progressive approach while meeting CySEC requirements. Sources: Robinhood onboarding metrics, DraftKings engagement data, gambling industry DAU benchmarks.
+35%
Barrier Futures Adoption
Projecting 35% of new users choose Barrier Futures for their first trade when the barrier slider is prominent and well-explained. In a market where $150B in forced liquidations occur annually, zero-liquidation products have massive demand.
Why this mattersIf barriers become the default entry point for beginners, Perpetuals has a moat no other exchange can easily copy. Polymarket proved binary “yes/no” simplicity can drive $3.5B in volume — our barrier slider applies the same principle to derivatives: choose your max loss, that’s it. It’s not just a feature, it’s a fundamentally different onboarding path. Sources: CoinGlass liquidation data, Polymarket peak volume (Nov 2024).
-60%
Support Tickets
Clear risk visualization, inline help tooltips, and proactive BayesShield warnings (11.7B trades analyzed, 92% manipulation filter rate) reduce “what happened to my money?” tickets by an estimated 60%.
AI-powered preventionBayesShield AI doesn’t just protect the platform — it protects the user. Proactive risk alerts (red accent, haptic feedback, 2-tap response flow) mean users get warned before bad things happen, not after. Personalized push notifications see +300% CTR vs generic alerts in gambling platforms. Sources: BayesShield internal metrics, gambling notification CTR benchmarks.

Content Strategy

Copy is UX. The difference between “Transaction Failed” and “Your order couldn’t go through — try reducing the amount” is the difference between a frustrated user and a retained one.

Error Messages
Tone: calm, specific, actionable. Never blame the user. Always suggest a next step.
“Error: Insufficient margin”
“You need $42 more to open this position. Reduce size or add funds.”
My ruleEvery error message must answer three questions: what happened, why, and what to do next. If it doesn’t answer all three, it’s not done.
Onboarding Copy
Tone: encouraging, plain English, no jargon. Assume zero knowledge. Build confidence.
“Complete KYC verification to enable trading functionality”
“Let’s verify your identity so you can start trading. Takes about 2 minutes.”
Trading Terminology
Progressive disclosure: use simple labels with “learn more” tooltips for jargon. Never force a beginner to Google something.
Order type: IOC / FOK / GTC
Market • Limit • Barrier   [More types →]
The jargon problemTrading has more jargon than medicine. You can’t eliminate it — Sarah needs “IOC” — but you can hide it from Alex until he’s ready.
Risk Warnings
Tone: direct, honest, not scary. Legally required (MiFID II Art. 24, ESMA mandated loss disclosure) but must be human-readable. No all-caps disclaimers.
“WARNING: LEVERAGED TRADING CARRIES SIGNIFICANT RISK OF LOSS”
“With this barrier, the most you can lose is $150. Your position closes automatically if the price hits $48,500. (74–89% of retail accounts lose money trading CFDs.)”
UKGC RTS 14 adapted for tradingGambling regulators require risk warnings to be “clear, fair, and not misleading” (UKGC RTS 14F). ESMA mandates percentage loss disclosure. We combine both: show the industry stat (74–89% lose) alongside the personal risk ($150 max loss with barriers). The contrast makes barriers feel empowering, not scary. Sources: UKGC Remote Technical Standards, ESMA Decision (EU) 2018/796.
Empty States
Tone: helpful, inviting. Empty screens are opportunities, not dead ends. Always suggest the next action.
[blank screen]
“No open positions yet. Ready for your first trade?” [Explore Markets]
Microcopy
Button labels, tooltips, confirmations. Every word counts. “Buy BTC” is better than “Submit Order” — specificity builds confidence.
“Submit” / “Cancel” / “OK”
“Buy 0.5 BTC” / “Go Back” / “Got it”
Notification strategy from gambling UXGambling platforms see +300% CTR from personalized vs generic push notifications, and +11% engagement from well-timed alerts. We apply this to trading: “BTC just hit your price target of $52,000” beats “Price alert triggered.” But we also follow the UKGC responsible design line — no “You’re missing out!” FOMO triggers, no loss-chasing nudges. Sources: Gambling push notification benchmarks, UKGC RTS 14 responsible messaging guidelines.

Prototype Notes

The stuff that separates a good app from a great one. Timing, easing, haptics, gestures — these details are invisible when they’re right and infuriating when they’re wrong. I obsess over these.

Button States

Default: Full opacity, standard background

Pressed: Scale 0.97, opacity 0.8 (100ms ease-out)

Disabled: 40% opacity, no pointer events

Loading: Pulse animation, spinner replaces text

Screen Transitions

Tab switch: Crossfade, 200ms ease

Push navigation: Slide left, 300ms cubic-bezier(0.16,1,0.3,1)

Modal open: Slide up from bottom + backdrop fade, 350ms

Modal close: Slide down + backdrop fade, 250ms

Gesture Support

Swipe left/right: Switch between Chart ↔ Order Book ↔ Trade Feed

Pull-to-refresh: Portfolio and market data screens

Long press: Position card → quick actions (close, edit, add TP/SL)

Pinch-to-zoom: Chart time frame adjustment

My thinkingThe swipe between chart/orderbook/feed is inspired by how Robinhood handles horizontal content, but adapted for a trading-dense layout. Long press on position cards is critical — it’s the fastest path to closing a position in a panic.

Real-Time Updates

Price ticks: Number flash green/red on change (300ms) — Stake.com-inspired color pulse

P&L updates: Smooth number counter animation (Kronos X feeds at <1ms latency)

Order book: Row slide-in/out for new/removed orders (1–2M+ orders/sec throughput)

Trade feed: New rows prepend with fade-in (150ms), Ledgera T+0 settlement confirmation

Infrastructure meets animationKronos X delivers 1–2M+ orders/sec at <1ms latency with 99.99% uptime. The UI must keep up: WebSocket streams feed price ticks, order book depth, and trade feed in real-time. Animation duration (150–300ms) is tuned to feel instant without causing visual noise. Betfair Exchange’s price ladder pattern influenced our order book rendering. Sources: Kronos X specs, Betfair Exchange UI analysis.

Loading States

Skeleton screens: Pulsing gray rectangles matching final layout

Chart loading: Skeleton with gradient shimmer

Data refresh: Subtle top-bar progress indicator

Order submission: Button → spinner → checkmark → dismiss

Animation Specs

Easing: cubic-bezier(0.16, 1, 0.3, 1) for primary

Micro: 100-200ms for UI feedback

Navigation: 250-350ms for screen transitions

Data: 300ms for number changes and chart updates

60fps minimum for all animations

Haptic Feedback

Order submitted: Medium impact

Order filled: Success notification

Barrier triggered: Heavy impact + warning

Slider snaps: Light impact at tick marks

Why haptics matter hereTrading is emotional. When your barrier gets hit, you need to feel it in your hand before you even read the screen. The heavy impact + warning combination is intentionally jarring — it’s the phone saying “something just happened to your money.”

Accessibility

VoiceOver: All interactive elements labeled

Dynamic Type: Supports iOS text scaling

Color contrast: WCAG AA minimum (4.5:1) — critical for financial data readability

Reduced motion: Respects system preference, disables price flash animations

Accessibility as responsible designUKGC RTS 14 requires gambling platforms to accommodate vulnerable users. We extend this: high-contrast mode for outdoor trading, reduced-motion for vestibular sensitivity, screen reader support for visually impaired traders. Accessibility isn’t a checkbox — it’s a competitive advantage when 15% of the global population has a disability. Sources: WCAG 2.1 guidelines, UKGC RTS 14 vulnerability provisions, WHO disability statistics.
