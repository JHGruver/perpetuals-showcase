Barrier Futures

No-liquidation derivatives that solve the $150B annual forced liquidation problem

The Problem

Traditional perpetual swaps have cascading liquidations: one position is liquidated, pushing the price further, which liquidates the next position, creating a chain reaction. In 2025 alone, $150 billion in forced liquidations occurred across crypto derivatives markets. ESMA data shows 74–89% of retail CFD/derivative accounts lose money.

How Barrier Futures Work

A Barrier Future closes automatically at a pre-set barrier price — the user's chosen risk limit. There are no margin calls, no funding fees, and no liquidation cascades. The barrier IS the risk limit. Users set it at entry and know their maximum possible loss upfront. Same leveraged exposure as a perpetual swap, fundamentally different risk profile.

For Brokers
A product they can offer clients without the reputation risk of liquidation cascades. Clean regulatory story under MiFID II. No need to explain why a client lost more than they deposited.
For Retail Traders
Leveraged exposure without the nightmare scenario. Maximum loss is known upfront. No midnight margin calls. Position simply closes at the barrier price.
For Regulators
A derivatives product that actually protects retail users. Directly addresses ESMA's concerns about retail CFD losses. First regulated product of its kind under an MTF license.
UX Design Opportunity
The barrier slider with real-time P&L simulation is the key interface. "If BTC drops to this price, your position closes. Your maximum loss is $X." Progressive disclosure of complexity — simple for beginners, full depth for professionals.

Why It Matters

Barrier Futures is a genuinely novel financial product. No one else has this under an MTF license. It's the primary reason Perpetuals exists — the product that justifies the entire regulatory and infrastructure investment.

How It Works

Ledgera is a meta-blockchain settlement layer that provides instant (T+0) cross-chain settlement. It supports multiple chains — ETH, BTC, USDC, and others — with real-time clearing and finality. No intermediary clearinghouse required.

The EU T+1 settlement mandate doesn't take effect until October 2027. Ledgera already operates at T+0, exceeding the requirement by over two years. This is a significant competitive and regulatory advantage.

Tokenized Securities Settlement
Ledgera provides the infrastructure for tokenized securities settlement — the same capability Gruhn pioneered at FTX Europe with the world's first regulatory-approved tokenized stocks. Tokenized securities are traditional financial instruments (stocks, bonds, ETFs) represented as blockchain tokens. This enables 24/7 trading, fractional ownership, and instant settlement of assets that traditionally take T+2 days to settle.
Consumer Brand: Ledgera Console
Ledgera also exists as a standalone consumer brand within the Perpetuals ecosystem. Background

The Grid Ledger System was developed by Satoshi Kobayashi and Hiroki Yamamoto at Earlyworks, prior to the Perpetual Markets merger. It's a proprietary hybrid blockchain that achieves 0.016-second transaction speeds.

GLS serves as the settlement backbone for the entire Perpetuals technology stack. The integration between GLS and Kronos X is what enables self-clearing — the ability to settle trades without a third-party central counterparty clearinghouse.

What "Hybrid Blockchain" Means
Pure Proof of Work (PoW): Bitcoin's model — miners compete to validate blocks. Extremely secure but slow (10 min blocks) and energy-intensive. Not suitable for exchange settlement.
Pure Proof of Stake (PoS): Ethereum's model — validators stake capital. Faster (12-15 sec blocks) but still too slow for institutional trading.
GLS Hybrid: Combines a permissioned validation layer (selected, trusted nodes for speed) with cryptographic finality guarantees (immutability once settled). Result: 0.016-second transactions with the security assurances financial regulators require. It's the speed of a centralized database with the auditability of a blockchain.
Enterprise Ethereum Alliance (EEA) Membership
The EEA is a consortium of 400+ enterprises including JP Morgan, Microsoft, Intel, and ConsenSys. Membership signals that GLS is enterprise-grade and interoperable with Ethereum standards. Benefits: access to enterprise specifications, credibility with institutional clients who require standards compliance, and interoperability with the broader Ethereum ecosystem (important for Ledgera's cross-chain settlement).
GLS + Ledgera Integration
GLS is the engine; Ledgera is the product built on it. GLS provides the raw blockchain infrastructure — transaction processing, consensus, and finality. Ledgera wraps this in settlement business logic: cross-chain coordination, tokenized security handling, and regulatory compliance checks. When a trade settles on Ledgera, the actual on-chain write happens on GLS. External chains (ETH, SOL) are bridged into GLS for unified settlement, then results are propagated back to native chains.

Trading Concepts Reference

Everything a designer needs to know about how trading works — order types, order books, and the matching engine.

Order Types

Every trade starts with an order. Understanding order types is essential — each one maps to a distinct UI pattern, confirmation flow, and risk profile.

Market Order
Executes immediately at the best available price. The simplest order type — "buy/sell now." The user sees a single button (Buy or Sell), no price input needed. Risk: price can slip between the time the user taps and the fill. UX note: Show estimated price + slippage warning. This is the default for beginners.
Limit Order
Executes only at a specified price or better. The user sets their desired price — "buy at $50,000 or lower." The order sits in the order book until it's matched or cancelled. UX note: Requires a price input field. Show how far the limit price is from the current market price. Most used order type by active traders.
Stop Order (Stop-Loss)
Becomes a market order when a trigger price is hit. Used to limit losses — "if price drops to $48,000, sell immediately." Doesn't guarantee the exact price, just that the order activates. UX note: Requires a trigger price input. Often shown as a secondary action on the position card.
Stop-Limit Order
Becomes a limit order (not market) when a trigger price is hit. Two prices: stop price (trigger) + limit price (worst acceptable fill). More control than a stop order, but risk of not filling if the market moves too fast. UX note: Two price fields = more complex form. Best for advanced users.
Take-Profit Order
Opposite of stop-loss. Closes a position at a target profit level. "If BTC reaches $55,000, sell and lock in profit." UX note: Often paired with stop-loss in an order ticket. Show projected P&L at the take-profit level.
Trailing Stop
A stop order that follows the price as it moves in the trader's favor. Set a trailing distance (e.g., $500 or 2%). If BTC goes from $50K to $55K, the stop trails up from $49.5K to $54.5K. If price reverses by the trail amount, the stop triggers. UX note: Needs a trail amount input (fixed or percentage). Visual showing the trailing relationship is highly effective.
OCO (One-Cancels-Other)
Two orders linked together — when one fills, the other is automatically cancelled. Typically pairs a take-profit with a stop-loss. "If BTC hits $55K, take profit. If it drops to $48K, stop loss." UX note: Two-panel order form or a bracket visualization around the current price.
Post-Only
Guarantees the order will only be added to the order book as a maker order (not matched immediately). If it would match, it's rejected instead. Used by traders who want to earn maker fee rebates. UX note: Checkbox or toggle on the order form. Show fee savings.
FOK (Fill or Kill)
The entire order must fill immediately and completely, or it's cancelled entirely. No partial fills allowed. Used for large institutional orders where partial execution creates risk. UX note: Dropdown under "Time in Force" on advanced order forms.
IOC (Immediate or Cancel)
Fill whatever you can immediately, cancel the rest. Unlike FOK, partial fills are OK. Used when speed matters more than complete fills. UX note: Another "Time in Force" option. Show estimated fill percentage.
GTC (Good Till Cancelled)
Order stays active until it's filled or manually cancelled. The default for limit orders. Can remain in the book for days, weeks, or months. UX note: Default behavior — may not need explicit UI. Show in order management as "active."

Order Book Mechanics

The order book is the core data structure of any exchange. It's where all unmatched limit orders live.

Bid & Ask
Bid = the highest price buyers are willing to pay. Ask = the lowest price sellers are willing to accept. The bid is always lower than the ask. These two prices are the most important numbers on any trading screen.
Spread
The difference between the best bid and best ask. A tight spread (e.g., $0.01) means high liquidity. A wide spread (e.g., $50) means low liquidity or a volatile market. UX note: Display spread prominently near the current price. Color-code: green for tight, yellow/red for wide.
Depth of Market (DOM)
The full list of all bid and ask orders at every price level. Shows how much volume exists at each price. "Depth" = how much the price would move if you placed a large order. Visualized as a depth chart (mountain chart) or as the Level 2 order book table.
Level 2 Data
The detailed order book showing individual price levels and their sizes. Level 1 = just best bid/ask. Level 2 = full depth. Professional traders rely on Level 2 to read market sentiment and detect large orders ("walls").
Maker vs. Taker
Maker = adds liquidity by placing a limit order that goes into the book. Taker = removes liquidity by placing an order that matches immediately. Makers typically pay lower fees (sometimes earn rebates). Takers pay higher fees. This is fundamental to exchange fee models.

The Matching Engine

From a user's perspective, the matching engine is invisible — but understanding it helps design better feedback states:

1. User submits an order (market, limit, etc.)

2. Kronos X engine receives it in microseconds

3. Engine checks: does this order match any resting order in the book?

4. If yes → instant fill (or partial fill). If no → order joins the book.

5. Priority: Price-Time. Best price wins. At same price, first order wins.

6. Fill confirmation sent back to the user.

Kronos X processes this in <50 microseconds. The bottleneck is never the engine — it's the network latency to the user.

Positions & Margin

How leveraged trading works — and why Barrier Futures change everything.

Long vs. Short Positions

Long = you profit when the price goes up. You're "buying" the asset. Short = you profit when the price goes down. You're "selling" an asset you don't own (borrowing it). Every derivatives exchange needs both sides. In the UI, this is the fundamental Buy/Sell (or Long/Short) toggle.

Leverage Explained

Leverage lets traders control a larger position with less capital. With 10x leverage, $1,000 controls a $10,000 position. Gains and losses are both multiplied.

2xConservative — $1K controls $2K. Common in spot margin.
5xModerate — $1K controls $5K. Popular with swing traders.
10xStandard perp default. A 10% move = 100% gain or total loss.
50xAggressive — a 2% adverse move liquidates the position.
100xMaximum on most exchanges. 1% move = total loss. Extremely risky.

Margin Types

Initial Margin
The collateral required to open a position. At 10x leverage, initial margin = 10% of position size. A $10,000 position requires $1,000 initial margin.
Maintenance Margin
The minimum collateral to keep a position open. Usually lower than initial margin (e.g., 5%). If your collateral drops below this, you get a margin call or are liquidated.
Margin Call
A warning that your collateral is approaching the maintenance margin. You must add more collateral or reduce the position. If you don't act, the exchange liquidates you. UX note: This is a critical alert state. Red banners, push notifications, urgent tone.
Isolated Margin
Each position has its own separate collateral. If one position is liquidated, your other positions and wallet balance are unaffected. Safer for managing individual trade risk. UX note: Per-position margin display. Popular with cautious traders.
Cross Margin
All positions share your entire account balance as collateral. Lower liquidation risk for individual positions (more cushion), but if one position goes bad, it can drain your entire account. UX note: Show total account health, not per-position.

P&L (Profit & Loss)

Unrealized P&L: The profit or loss on an open position, calculated against the current mark price. Changes in real time. Also called "floating" P&L. Displayed on every position card.

Realized P&L: The actual profit or loss from closed positions. Locked in when you close. This is your "real" performance. Shown in trade history and portfolio summary.

Price Types

Last PricePrice of the most recent trade executed
Mark PriceFair price calculated from multiple sources. Used for liquidations to prevent manipulation.
Index PriceWeighted average across multiple exchanges. The "true" spot price reference.

How Barrier Futures Change This

Barrier Futures eliminate the entire margin system.

No initial margin. No maintenance margin. No margin calls. No liquidation cascades. No funding rates.

Instead: you set a barrier price (your maximum loss level). The position auto-closes cleanly at that price. Your risk is known and fixed from the moment you enter.

This is the core innovation — it removes the most dangerous and confusing aspects of leveraged trading and replaces them with a single, intuitive concept: "How much are you willing to lose?"

Chart & Market Data Anatomy

The visual language of trading — candlesticks, indicators, and how to read market data screens.

Candlestick Structure: OHLCV

Every candlestick on a trading chart represents a time period and contains five data points:

Open (O)Price at the start of the period
High (H)Highest price during the period
Low (L)Lowest price during the period
Close (C)Price at the end of the period
Volume (V)Total amount traded during the period

Green/bullish candle: Close > Open (price went up). Red/bearish candle: Close < Open (price went down). The body = Open to Close range. The wicks = High and Low beyond the body.

Time Frames

Charts can show candles at different intervals. Each candle = one period:

1m, 5m, 15mScalping / day trading. Very short-term.
1H, 4HIntraday analysis. Popular with active traders.
1D (Daily)Swing trading. Most common for technical analysis.
1W (Weekly)Long-term trends. Institutional perspective.

UX note: Time frame selector is one of the most-tapped controls. It should be always visible, not hidden in a menu.

Common Indicators

Moving Averages (MA / EMA)
A smoothed line showing average price over N periods. Simple MA (SMA) = equal weight. Exponential MA (EMA) = more weight on recent prices. Common periods: 20, 50, 200. When price crosses above the 200 MA = bullish signal. Overlaid directly on the price chart.
RSI (Relative Strength Index)
Momentum oscillator from 0-100. Above 70 = overbought (might drop). Below 30 = oversold (might rise). Shown as a separate panel below the main chart. Default period: 14.
Bollinger Bands
Three lines: middle = 20-period SMA. Upper/lower bands = 2 standard deviations above/below. Price near upper band = potentially overbought. Price squeezing between bands = volatility about to increase. Overlaid on the price chart.
VWAP (Volume Weighted Average Price)
Average price weighted by volume. Institutional benchmark — "did I get a good price?" If you bought below VWAP, you got a better-than-average deal. Resets daily. Overlaid on intraday charts.

Depth Chart

The depth chart is a mountain-shaped visualization of the order book. Left side (green) = cumulative buy orders. Right side (red) = cumulative sell orders. The meeting point = current price.

Steep walls = strong support/resistance. Thin areas = price could move quickly through them. Institutional traders watch for "whale walls" — large orders that act as price barriers.

UX note: Often displayed as a tab alongside the order book table. Interactive: hover to see price/volume at any level.

Trade Feed / Ticker

A real-time stream of every trade as it happens. Each entry shows:

• Price • Size • Time • Side (buy/sell)

Green rows = trades at the ask (buyer initiated). Red rows = trades at the bid (seller initiated). Fast-scrolling feed = high activity. Used by scalpers to read "tape" (flow of orders).

UX note: Auto-scrolling list. Recent trades at top. Color-coded. Optional: aggregate by time or show individual fills.

Trade Lifecycle

From order entry to settlement — every step of a trade mapped to Perpetuals infrastructure and the screens a designer builds.

STEP 1

Pre-Trade

Order Entry: User selects asset, order type, size, leverage/barrier level. The order form is the most critical screen in any trading app.

Risk Checks: BayesShield AI scores the trade in real-time. Position limits checked. Margin requirements calculated (or barrier price validated).

Screens: Order ticket, risk summary, order confirmation modal

STEP 2

Matching

Kronos X Engine: Order enters the matching engine. Price-time priority — best price first, earliest time wins ties.

Order Book Interaction: Market orders match immediately against resting limit orders. Limit orders either match or join the book.

Screens: Order book (real-time), trade feed showing new matches

STEP 3

Execution

Fill Confirmation: Trade executes. User receives confirmation with fill price, quantity, fees, and timestamp.

Partial Fills: Large orders may fill across multiple price levels. The UI must show partial fill progress and weighted average fill price.

Screens: Fill notification toast, order status update, blotter entry

STEP 4

Clearing & Settlement

Ledgera Settlement: T+0 instant settlement. Assets and obligations are settled on-chain immediately after matching.

Self-Clearing via GLS: No third-party CCP. The Grid Ledger System processes settlement in 0.016 seconds. This is invisible to the user but enables the "instant" experience.

Screens: Settlement confirmation (often merged with fill confirmation)

STEP 5

Post-Trade

Position Management: Open position appears in the portfolio. P&L updates in real time. Stop-loss / take-profit orders can be attached.

Monitoring: BayesShield continues to score risk. Barrier proximity alerts for Barrier Futures. Portfolio-level risk metrics updated.

Screens: Position list, P&L dashboard, order history, trade report

STEP 6

Close / Expiry

Manual Close: User closes position via the position card. Realized P&L calculated and credited.

Barrier Trigger: For Barrier Futures, position auto-closes at barrier price. Clean exit, no cascading effects.

Reporting: Trade added to history. Tax lot tracking. Performance analytics updated.

Screens: Close confirmation, trade history, performance report

Trading Knowledge

Fee Structure

How exchange fees work and how they surface in the trading UX.

Maker / Taker Model

Most exchanges charge different fees based on whether you add or remove liquidity:

Maker fee0.01% – 0.05% (or even negative = rebate)
Taker fee0.03% – 0.10%
Why different?Makers improve liquidity, takers consume it

Volume-Based Fee Tiers

Exchanges typically offer lower fees for higher trading volume. Tiers are based on 30-day rolling volume:

Tier 1<$1M/month — highest fees (standard retail)
Tier 2-5$1M–$50M/month — progressive discounts
VIP / MM>$50M/month — custom rates, possible rebates

Other Fees

Funding Rates (Perpetual Futures)
Periodic payments between longs and shorts to keep the contract anchored to spot price. Paid every 8 hours on most exchanges. Barrier Futures eliminate this entirely.
Withdrawal Fees
Fixed fee per withdrawal, varies by network. On-chain gas fees are passed through. PerpetualPay handles fiat withdrawals with their own fee schedule.
Settlement / Clearing Fees
Most exchanges pass through CCP clearing fees. Perpetuals' self-clearing eliminates third-party clearing costs entirely.

UX Implications

Order entry: Show estimated fees before confirmation. "Est. fee: $0.50 (0.05%)" next to the total.

Fee tier display: Show current tier, volume to next tier, and potential savings. Gamification opportunity.

Post-only indicator: When a user selects Post-Only, show the maker fee they'll pay (or rebate they'll earn).

Trade confirmation: Break down: execution price, fee, net cost. Never surprise the user with hidden fees.

Glossary

80+ terms organized by category — from regulatory frameworks to trading mechanics to crypto-native concepts.

Regulatory & Compliance

MTF
Multilateral Trading Facility. A regulated trading venue under MiFID II. Same classification as traditional stock exchanges.
MiFID II
Markets in Financial Instruments Directive II. EU regulation governing investment services. Enables cross-border passporting.
MiCA
Markets in Crypto-Assets. Comprehensive EU crypto regulation. Full enforcement July 1, 2026.
DORA
Digital Operational Resilience Act. EU regulation requiring ICT risk management and resilience testing for financial entities.
EMIR
European Market Infrastructure Regulation. Rules for OTC derivatives, central counterparties, and trade repositories.
CySEC
Cyprus Securities and Exchange Commission. The regulatory authority that issued Perpetuals' MTF license.
ESMA
European Securities and Markets Authority. EU-level regulatory body. Issues guidance on CFD restrictions and investor protection.
Passporting
Using a license from one EU/EEA country to operate across all 30 member states without additional licenses.
KYC/AML
Know Your Customer / Anti-Money Laundering. Identity verification and transaction monitoring requirements.
GDPR
General Data Protection Regulation. EU data privacy law governing how personal data is collected, stored, and processed.

Trading Mechanics

Market Order
An order to buy or sell immediately at the best available price. Fastest execution, but price is not guaranteed.
Limit Order
An order to buy or sell at a specified price or better. Sits in the order book until matched. Price guaranteed, execution is not.
Stop Order
An order that becomes a market order when a trigger price is reached. Used for stop-losses. Also called a "stop-loss order."
Stop-Limit
An order that becomes a limit order (not market) when the trigger price is hit. Two prices: stop price + limit price. More control, risk of no fill.
OCO
One-Cancels-Other. Two linked orders where executing one automatically cancels the other. Common: stop-loss + take-profit pair.
Post-Only
Order that only adds liquidity. If it would match immediately, it's rejected. Ensures maker fee (lower) instead of taker fee.
FOK
Fill or Kill. Entire order must execute immediately and completely, or it's cancelled. No partial fills allowed.
IOC
Immediate or Cancel. Fill what you can immediately, cancel the rest. Partial fills are acceptable.
GTC
Good Till Cancelled. Order remains active until filled or manually cancelled. The default for most limit orders.
Bid
The highest price a buyer is willing to pay. The "buy" side of the order book.
Ask
The lowest price a seller is willing to accept. The "sell" side of the order book. Also called the "offer."
Spread
The difference between the best bid and best ask. Tight spread = high liquidity. Wide spread = low liquidity or high volatility.
Maker
A trader who adds liquidity by placing a limit order that rests in the book. Makers typically pay lower fees.
Taker
A trader who removes liquidity by placing an order that matches immediately. Takers pay higher fees.
Slippage
The difference between the expected price and the actual fill price. Occurs with market orders in fast-moving or illiquid markets.
Price Impact
How much a single order moves the market price. Large orders in thin order books have high price impact.
CLOB
Central Limit Order Book. The standard order matching model used by exchanges. Orders sorted by price then time. Kronos X uses a CLOB.

Positions & Margin

Long
A position that profits when the price increases. "Going long" = buying with the expectation of selling higher.
Short
A position that profits when the price decreases. "Going short" = selling borrowed assets to buy back cheaper later.
Leverage
Borrowing to amplify position size. 10x leverage means $1K controls $10K. Multiplies both gains and losses.
Initial Margin
The collateral required to open a leveraged position. At 10x leverage, initial margin = 10% of position value.
Maintenance Margin
Minimum collateral to keep a position open. Drop below this = margin call or liquidation.
Margin Call
Warning that collateral is near the maintenance margin. Add more funds or reduce position to avoid liquidation.
Isolated Margin
Each position has its own separate collateral. Liquidation of one position doesn't affect others. Safer per-trade.
Cross Margin
All positions share the full account balance as collateral. Less likely to be liquidated, but one bad trade can drain everything.
Unrealized P&L
Profit or loss on open positions, calculated against mark price. Changes in real time. Also called "floating P&L."
Realized P&L
Actual profit or loss from closed positions. Locked in when the position is closed.
Mark Price
Fair price calculated from multiple sources. Used for P&L calculations and liquidation triggers to prevent manipulation.
Index Price
Weighted average price across multiple exchanges. Reference "true" spot price. Used to calculate mark price.
Liquidation
Forced closure of a leveraged position when margin is insufficient. Cascading liquidations are the $150B problem Barrier Futures solve.

Derivatives

Perpetual Futures
Derivative contracts with no expiry date. Traders can hold positions indefinitely, paying periodic funding rates.
Barrier Futures
Perpetuals' novel derivative. Position auto-closes at a pre-set barrier price. No margin calls, no funding fees, no cascading liquidations.
Funding Rate
Periodic payment between long and short positions in perpetual futures. Keeps the contract price anchored to spot. Barrier Futures eliminate this.
Open Interest
Total value of outstanding derivative contracts. Peak crypto OI reached $235.9B in October 2025.
Delta
Rate of change of an option's price relative to the underlying asset's price. Delta of 0.5 = option moves $0.50 per $1 move in the asset.
Gamma
Rate of change of delta. Measures how fast delta changes as the underlying moves. High gamma = delta is unstable.
Theta
Time decay. How much an option's value decreases per day as expiry approaches. All options lose value over time.
Vega
Sensitivity to volatility. How much an option's price changes per 1% change in implied volatility.
Strike Price
The price at which an option can be exercised. For Barrier Futures, analogous to the barrier level.
Expiry
The date a derivative contract expires. Perpetual futures have no expiry. Standard futures/options have fixed expiry dates.
ITM / OTM
In-The-Money / Out-of-The-Money. ITM = option would be profitable if exercised now. OTM = it would not be.
Notional Value
The total value of a leveraged position. With 10x leverage on $1K, notional value = $10K.
Contract Size
The amount of underlying asset one contract represents. E.g., 1 BTC futures contract = 1 BTC.
Tick Size
Minimum price increment for an instrument. BTC/USD might have a tick size of $0.10.
Basis
The difference between futures price and spot price. Positive basis = futures trading above spot (contango).
Contango
When futures price > spot price. Normal in most markets. Means futures traders pay a premium for future delivery.
Backwardation
When futures price < spot price. Unusual. Can signal supply shortages or bearish long-term sentiment.

Charts & Technical Analysis

OHLCV
Open, High, Low, Close, Volume. The five data points in every candlestick. The fundamental unit of chart data.
Candlestick
A chart element showing price movement over a time period. Body = open-to-close range. Wicks = high and low extremes.
Time Frame
The duration each candle represents. Common: 1m, 5m, 15m, 1H, 4H, 1D, 1W. Shorter = more noise, longer = smoother trends.
Volume
The total amount of an asset traded in a given period. High volume confirms price moves. Low volume suggests weak conviction.
VWAP
Volume Weighted Average Price. Average price weighted by volume. Institutional benchmark for "good" execution quality.
Moving Average
Smoothed price line over N periods. SMA = equal weight. EMA = recent-weighted. Common: 20, 50, 200 period.
RSI
Relative Strength Index. Oscillator 0-100. Above 70 = overbought. Below 30 = oversold. Default: 14 periods.
Bollinger Bands
Three lines: SMA ± 2 standard deviations. Price squeezing between bands = volatility expansion imminent.

Market Structure

Spot Price
The current market price for immediate delivery. The "real" price of an asset right now.
Mark-to-Market
Revaluing open positions to current market prices. Determines unrealized P&L and margin requirements.
Liquidity
How easily an asset can be bought/sold without moving the price. Deep order books = high liquidity.
Volatility
The degree of price fluctuation. High volatility = big price swings. Crypto is significantly more volatile than traditional assets.
Circuit Breaker
Automatic trading halt triggered by extreme price moves. Prevents flash crashes. Common in TradFi, rare in crypto.
Dark Pool
A private exchange where large orders execute without showing in the public order book. Prevents price impact on large trades.
Price Discovery
The process by which the market determines the fair price of an asset through supply and demand.
Market Depth
The volume of orders at each price level in the order book. Deep market = large orders can fill without significant price impact.

Crypto-Specific

CEX
Centralized Exchange. An exchange operated by a company (Binance, Coinbase, Perpetuals). Custodial — the exchange holds user funds.
DEX
Decentralized Exchange. An exchange running on smart contracts (Uniswap, dYdX). Non-custodial — users control their own funds.
AMM
Automated Market Maker. DEX model where liquidity pools replace order books. Uses math formulas (x*y=k) instead of matching engines.
Stablecoin
A crypto token pegged to a stable asset (usually USD). USDC and USDT are the most common. Used as trading collateral and settlement currency.
Gas Fees
Transaction fees on blockchain networks. Paid to validators for processing transactions. Varies by network congestion.
On-chain / Off-chain
On-chain = recorded on the blockchain (transparent, permanent). Off-chain = processed outside the blockchain (faster, cheaper). Exchange matching is off-chain; settlement is on-chain.
Oracle
A service that feeds real-world data (prices, events) to smart contracts. Price oracles are critical for mark price calculations and liquidation triggers.

Risk Management

VaR
Value at Risk. Statistical measure of the maximum expected loss over a time period at a given confidence level. E.g., "1-day 99% VaR of $10M."
Stress Test
Simulating extreme market scenarios to test system resilience. Required by DORA for EU-regulated firms.
Hedging
Taking an offsetting position to reduce risk. E.g., holding long BTC spot and short BTC futures to be "market neutral."
Insurance Fund
A pool of capital that covers losses from bankrupt positions. Prevents socialized losses among other traders.
Socialized Loss
When losses from liquidated positions are spread across profitable traders. Happens when the insurance fund is depleted. Very unpopular.

Institutional & Infrastructure

Prime Brokerage
A suite of services for institutional clients: margin lending, trade execution, custody, reporting. The "full service" tier.
DMA
Direct Market Access. Institutional traders connecting directly to the matching engine, bypassing standard UI. Lowest latency.
HFT
High-Frequency Trading. Algorithmic trading at microsecond speeds. Requires co-location and DMA. Kronos X's <50μs latency supports HFT.
FIX Protocol
Financial Information eXchange. Industry-standard protocol for electronic trading communication. Used by institutional clients to connect to exchanges.
SOR
Smart Order Router. Automatically routes orders to the best venue (exchange) for the best price/liquidity.
T+0 / T+1
Settlement timing. T+0 = instant settlement. T+1 = settlement one day after trade. EU mandates T+1 by Oct 2027. Ledgera = T+0.
Self-clearing
Ability to settle trades without a third-party clearinghouse (CCP). Enabled by Kronos X + GLS integration.
GLS
Grid Ledger System. Kobayashi's proprietary hybrid blockchain with 0.016s transaction speed.
HSM
Hardware Security Module. Physical device for managing cryptographic keys. Used in Perpetuals Vault.
Co-location
Placing trading servers physically next to the exchange's matching engine. Equinix FR2 in Frankfurt. Minimizes network latency.
